Why This Matters

Colorado's Proposition 123 created $300M+ in housing funds, released in 3-year cycles. To access these funds, Delta must adopt a "Fast Track" program by January 2027.

If Delta adopts Fast Track, local projects become eligible for grants and low-interest loans. If not, these funds go to other communities.

What the funding can be used for

Program What It Funds
Land Banking Grants to acquire land for future housing ($73M+ awarded)
Concessionary Debt Low-interest construction loans (3% vs. 7-8% market rate)
Down Payment Assistance Grants to help buyers purchase homes
Equity Program Investments in affordable rental developments ($67M+ awarded)
Mobile Home Programs Support for mobile home community preservation and improvements
Planning Capacity Up to $200K for staff/consultants to process applications faster

Who can apply?

Local governments, housing authorities, nonprofits, community land trusts, and private developers (for some programs).

Key deadline: Delta must adopt Fast Track by January 2027 to remain eligible for the next 3-year funding cycle (2027-2029).

Throughout this worksheet, we reference four Colorado communities that have officially adopted Fast Track (verified by DOLA). Each offers a different approach:

1 How long should units stay affordable?

This is the length of the deed restriction on affordable units.

What happens when the restriction expires?

When a deed restriction ends, the owner can convert to market-rate and capture full appreciation—sometimes called an "owner windfall." Longer terms reduce this concern.

Typical rental property lifetime

Most rental properties last 30-50+ years. Federal programs (HOME, LIHTC) require 20-30 year minimums. If restrictions expire too soon, the community loses affordable units while the buildings are still standing.

What our comparison communities use

  • Grand Junction: 30 years minimum—required for any project receiving fee waivers
  • Montrose: Not specified in their Fast Track resolution
  • Salida: Permanent—deed restrictions stay with the property "for generations"
  • De Beque: Not specified in their resolution—follows state baseline
Key trade-off: Shorter terms may be more attractive to builders, but longer terms provide more lasting community benefit and often qualify for better state/federal funding.

2 What % of units must be affordable to qualify?

State requires 50%. Delta's draft lowers this to attract more projects.

Why consider a lower threshold?

A 4-unit project (small local builder) at 50% = 2 affordable units. At 25% = 1 affordable unit. The lower bar may be the difference between a local builder participating or not.

What this means in practice

  • 4-unit project at 25%: 1 affordable unit, 3 market-rate
  • 4-unit project at 50%: 2 affordable units, 2 market-rate
  • 10-unit project at 25%: 2-3 affordable, 7-8 market-rate
  • 10-unit project at 50%: 5 affordable, 5 market-rate

What our comparison communities require

  • Grand Junction: 50% for Fast Track incentives (follows state baseline)
  • Montrose: 50% (follows state baseline)
  • Salida: ~17% (1 in 6 units) for all projects with 5+ units; 50% for Fast Track expedited review
  • De Beque: 50% (follows state baseline)

Note: Salida requires affordable units in all larger projects, not just those seeking incentives. Most communities follow the state's 50% threshold.

Key trade-off: Lower threshold = more projects may participate but fewer affordable units per project. Higher threshold = more affordable units per project but fewer projects may pencil out.

3 Should Delta offer a density bonus?

A density bonus lets builders construct more units than zoning allows. It's not city money—it's permission.

How it works

A density bonus allows builders to add units beyond what zoning normally allows. In exchange, they provide some affordable units. The city doesn't spend money—it grants permission.

What our comparison communities do

  • Grand Junction: Density bonus as part of Affordable Housing Incentive Program—developers can build more units in exchange for including affordable homes, combined with fee waivers
  • Montrose: REDO overlay district allows increased density near downtown—minimum lot size of 2,075 sq ft, additional units on standard lots, reduced parking
  • Salida: Density bonus for inclusionary housing—developers who include affordable units get density bonuses, parking reductions, and dimensional flexibility (2018 ordinance)
  • De Beque: No density bonus (bare-bones compliance)

A concrete example from Longmont, CO

25% density bonus for projects that make 12% of units affordable (at 80% AMI for sale, 50% AMI for rental). This means a 10-unit project becomes 12-13 units, with 1-2 affordable.

This costs the city nothing—it's just permission to build more. The developer gets more units to sell; the community gets affordable units.

Why offer it?

More total housing gets built. The builder gets more units to sell or rent. The city gets more affordable units. No taxpayer dollars change hands.

Example: What each bonus level means for a 10-unit project

Option 10 units becomes... Extra affordable
No bonus 10 units —
10% 11 units +0-1
20% (draft) 12 units +1
30% 13 units +1-2
40% 14 units +2
50% 15 units +2-3
Key trade-off: Buildings may be larger than current zoning envisions. This trades some neighborhood character for more housing supply and affordability.

4 What % of bonus units should be affordable?

This decision only matters if you chose a density bonus above

When a builder gets permission to add extra units, what share of those bonus units should be affordable?

Example: 20% density bonus on a 10-unit project

  • Builder gets 2 bonus units (10 × 20% = 2)
  • At 25% of bonus affordable: 0-1 affordable bonus unit
  • At 50% of bonus affordable: 1 affordable bonus unit
  • At 100% of bonus affordable: 2 affordable bonus units

How other programs structure this

Some programs require 100% of bonus units be affordable—the logic being that the developer is getting "free" extra units, so all of them should benefit the community. Others allow a lower percentage to make the deal more attractive to builders.

Delta has flexibility to design this however it wants. None of our comparison communities have documented requirements for bonus unit affordability.

Key trade-off: Higher requirements = more community benefit per project. Lower requirements = more attractive to builders, potentially more projects participate.

5 Should Delta offer fee waivers?

Fee waivers are optional incentives. This is the only part that costs the city money.

What fees are involved?

  • Planning fees: ~$500 application fee
  • Building permit fees: Based on project value (formula in fee schedule)
  • Tap fees: Water and sewer connection fees (~$3,200/unit)
  • Use tax: 3% of construction materials cost

What this costs the city

  • 4-unit project: ~$2,000 (moderate) to ~$5,500 (full)
  • 10-unit project: ~$6,000 (moderate) to ~$26,000 (full)
  • 20-unit project: ~$12,000 (moderate) to ~$84,000 (full)

Per household: A 10-unit project at "Full" incentives costs about $7 per Delta household (~3,700 total households).

The city only pays if housing gets built

Unlike grants or up-front spending, fee waivers only cost money when a qualifying project is completed. No project = no cost.

What our comparison communities offer

  • Grand Junction: 100% of impact fees waived for projects at ≤60% AMI; 75% waived at 61-80% AMI; water/sewer assistance also available
  • Montrose: REDO district reduces ADU tap fees from ~$6,800 to $300; no broad fee waivers for other housing types
  • Salida: Density bonus + parking/dimensional flexibility for inclusionary projects; deferred tap fee collection (not waiver)
  • De Beque: No fee waivers (bare-bones compliance—just 90-day review)

Fee waivers are optional. Grand Junction offers the most; De Beque offers none. Delta can land anywhere on this spectrum.

What each option costs the city

Option City offers 4-unit cost 10-unit cost
None 90-day review only Staff time Staff time
Moderate + planning & permit waivers ~$2,000 ~$6,000
Full All above + tap fees + use tax ~$5,500 ~$26,000

The city only gives up fees if housing gets built. No project = no cost.

Key trade-off: More incentives make Delta more competitive for builders and may unlock additional state funding. But incentives cost real taxpayer dollars when projects are built.
